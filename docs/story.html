Census Classification, Language of Instruction, and Educational Inequality

Author

Chandni Naidu Deadwyler

Published

December 31, 2024

Abstract

This paper examines whether gender gaps in literacy vary across different linguistic representation configurations in India. Using 2011 census data for 628 districts, categorized into three groups based on census representation patterns, I use ANOVA and post-hoc tests to analyze variation in gender literacy gaps. Category 1 includes states that use English as official language and medium of instruction; Category 2 includes states with indigenous regional languages and accurate census representation; Category 3 includes Hindi states where census categories mask substantial linguistic diversity. Results show that gender gaps are significantly larger in Category 3 (20.96 percentage points) compared to Category 2 (13.86 points) and Category 1 (9.83 points). These findings suggest that census classification systems that obscure linguistic distance between home and school language may correlate with educational gender inequality, with implications for India’s National Education Policy 2020.

1 Literature Review

India is one of the most linguistically diverse regions in the world (Mohanty, 2019). According to the 1961 census, 1,652 languages or dialects were spoken, though definitions of languages, dialects, and mother tongues have changed frequently in response to political tensions (Tieri, 2024). The current 2011 census reports 121 distinct languages, but only 22 are constitutionally recognized in the Eighth Schedule. This discrepancy creates challenges for educational planning.

Policy and Census Context

India’s language policy emerged from intense post-independence debates. The Constitution of 1950 established a 15-year transition during which Hindi would replace English as the sole official language after 1965. However, states with strong linguistic identity resisted. The anti-Hindi agitations of the 1960s forced a political compromise (Forrester, 1966).

The Official Languages Act (1963, amended 1967) guaranteed permanent co-official status for both Hindi and English at the Union level (Brass, 1974). The Eighth Schedule originally contained 14 languages (1950) and expanded to 22. Language inclusion criteria have been explicitly political rather than linguistic. Manipuri (1.7 million speakers) and Konkani (2.5 million) enjoy official status, while Bhojpuri (50+ million) and Rajasthani (25+ million) remain excluded (Annamalai, 2001).

The 2011 census recorded 19,569 distinct mother tongue returns and classified 93% as “others” or grouped under 121 languages. This massive aggregation obscures local linguistic diversity (Mohanty, 2019).

Language of Instruction and Learning Outcomes

Mother tongue instruction has positive effects in low and middle-income countries. Nakamura et al. (2023) found effect sizes of 0.3 to 0.75 SD in 45 studies. These findings align with Cummins’ (1979) developmental interdependence hypothesis. In Ethiopia, mother tongue instruction improved mathematics test scores by 0.159 SD (Seid, 2019).

Gender Effects in Education

The PIRLS assessment found girls outperforming boys in 51 of 57 countries (IEA, 2023). In India, we see a reversal: female literacy stands at 66% compared to 83% for males. Azam and Kingdon (2013) found pro-male bias in education expenditure. However, son preference alone does not explain India’s persistent gender gaps, as China and South Korea show no comparable gaps despite similar cultural patterns.

Research Gap

Research on mother tongue instruction and gender differences exists, but little work examines whether census representation intersects with gender inequalities. Balakrishnan et al. (2023) found marginally higher mathematics improvements for girls but did not investigate regional linguistic context.

2 Conceptual Framework

Category 1: English Official — States where English serves as official language (Arunachal Pradesh, Meghalaya, Nagaland, Sikkim). Census accurately records diverse tribal languages.

Category 2: Indigenous Languages (Accurate Census) — States where census, home, and school languages align: Kerala (99.82% alignment), Tamil Nadu (99.8%), West Bengal (98.91%), Karnataka (99.54%), Maharashtra (99.73%), Gujarat (99.18%).

Category 3: Hindi (Masked Diversity) — Hindi has 528 million speakers but only 322 million (61%) returned Hindi. The remaining 206 million speak Bhojpuri, Rajasthani, Awadhi, Maithili, etc.

data <- data %>%
  mutate(lang_cat = case_when(state_official_language == "English" ~ "1",
                              state_official_language == "Hindi" ~ "3", TRUE ~ "2"),
         lang_cat_label = factor(case_when(lang_cat == "1" ~ "Category 1: English",
                                           lang_cat == "2" ~ "Category 2: Indigenous",
                                           lang_cat == "3" ~ "Category 3: Hindi"),
                                 levels = c("Category 1: English", "Category 2: Indigenous", "Category 3: Hindi")),
         gender_gap = literacy_male_num - literacy_female_num)

Variables

Dependent Variable: Gender gap = male literacy % - female literacy %

Independent Variable: Census representation category (1, 2, or 3)

Methods

One-way ANOVA with Tukey’s HSD post-hoc tests.

Sex-Disaggregated Analysis

Male literacy: Indigenous districts exceed English by 6.7 pp; Hindi shows no significant difference from English (p = 0.958).

Female literacy: Hindi lags English by 11.6 pp and Indigenous by 14.2 pp; no significant difference between English and Indigenous (p = 0.431).

The gender gap stems primarily from depressed female literacy in Hindi districts.

6 Discussion

This study found strong evidence that census representation categories correlate with gender inequality in literacy. Gender gaps are more than twice as large in Hindi states (20.96 pp) compared to English states (9.83 pp). States with indigenous languages show intermediate gaps (13.86 pp). All differences are statistically significant (p < .001).

Interpretation

The correlation between census-masked diversity and larger gender gaps suggests several possibilities. In Hindi states, many students categorized as ‘Hindi mother tongue’ actually speak variants like Bhojpuri, Rajasthani, or Awadhi that differ substantially from Standard Hindi. When girls face additional barriers in patriarchal contexts—less encouragement for school attendance, higher domestic labor expectations, earlier marriage pressure—the added linguistic barrier may disproportionately affect their educational persistence.

Category 1 states, despite using English (a non-mother-tongue for most), show smallest gaps. This may be because explicit linguistic distance leads to explicit policy accommodations, or because northeastern states have different cultural patterns regarding gender and education.

These results have implications for India’s National Education Policy 2020. The policy’s assumption that census categories accurately represent ‘mother tongue’ is demonstrably false for 206 million speakers in Hindi states. Without recognizing this census masking, mother-tongue instruction mandates may inadvertently reinforce existing inequalities.

Limitations

Several limitations affect interpretation:

  1. Cross-sectional analysis cannot establish causation
  2. Census representation categories may correlate with other regional characteristics
  3. District-level aggregation masks individual variation
  4. Literacy is a minimal threshold that doesn’t capture reading comprehension or learning quality

7 Conclusion

Gender gaps in literacy vary systematically across India’s census representation categories, with the largest gaps (20.96 pp) where “Hindi” masks linguistic diversity. Sex-disaggregated analysis reveals that male literacy varies modestly (range = 76.47%–83.61%), while female literacy in Hindi-masked contexts (55.51%) falls 14.2 pp below indigenous language districts and 11.6 pp below English districts.

This pattern indicates that census-masked linguistic distance and patriarchal restrictions compound barriers for girls. Boys and men likely acquire Standard Hindi through unrestricted access to public domains, while girls face compounded disadvantages.

The NEP 2020’s assumption of census accuracy fails for 206 million speakers classified as “Hindi” while speaking different varieties. Effective policy requires transparent census practices and explicit recognition that apparent educational failure can be addressed by bridging linguistic gaps.

## Discussion

This study found strong evidence that census representation categories correlate with gender inequality in literacy. Gender gaps are more than twice as large in Hindi states (20.96 pp) compared to English states (9.83 pp). States with indigenous languages show intermediate gaps (13.86 pp). All differences are statistically significant (p < .001).

### Interpretation {.unnumbered}

The correlation between census-masked diversity and larger gender gaps suggests several possibilities. In Hindi states, many students categorized as 'Hindi mother tongue' actually speak variants like Bhojpuri, Rajasthani, or Awadhi that differ substantially from Standard Hindi. When girls face additional barriers in patriarchal contexts—less encouragement for school attendance, higher domestic labor expectations, earlier marriage pressure—the added linguistic barrier may disproportionately affect their educational persistence.

Category 1 states, despite using English (a non-mother-tongue for most), show smallest gaps. This may be because explicit linguistic distance leads to explicit policy accommodations, or because northeastern states have different cultural patterns regarding gender and education.

These results have implications for India's National Education Policy 2020. The policy's assumption that census categories accurately represent 'mother tongue' is demonstrably false for 206 million speakers in Hindi states. Without recognizing this census masking, mother-tongue instruction mandates may inadvertently reinforce existing inequalities.

### Limitations {.unnumbered}

Several limitations affect interpretation:

1. Cross-sectional analysis cannot establish causation
2. Census representation categories may correlate with other regional characteristics
3. District-level aggregation masks individual variation
4. Literacy is a minimal threshold that doesn't capture reading comprehension or learning quality

## Conclusion

Gender gaps in literacy vary systematically across India's census representation categories, with the largest gaps (20.96 pp) where "Hindi" masks linguistic diversity. Sex-disaggregated analysis reveals that male literacy varies modestly (range = 76.47%–83.61%), while female literacy in Hindi-masked contexts (55.51%) falls 14.2 pp below indigenous language districts and 11.6 pp below English districts.

This pattern indicates that census-masked linguistic distance and patriarchal restrictions compound barriers for girls. Boys and men likely acquire Standard Hindi through unrestricted access to public domains, while girls face compounded disadvantages.

The NEP 2020's assumption of census accuracy fails for 206 million speakers classified as "Hindi" while speaking different varieties. Effective policy requires transparent census practices and explicit recognition that apparent educational failure can be addressed by bridging linguistic gaps.
